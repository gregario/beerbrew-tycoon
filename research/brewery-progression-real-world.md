# Real-World Brewery Progression Analysis

How real breweries grow from kitchen experiments to global brands, with Irish/UK examples.
Purpose: Inform BeerBrew Tycoon's expansion stages, endgame paths, and business simulation.

---

## The 7 Stages of Brewery Growth

### Stage 0: Kitchen Brewer (The Hobbyist)
**Scale:** 5-25 litre batches
**Equipment:** Stovetop pot, plastic fermenter, bottles. Extract or simple BIAB kit
**Cost:** $50-200
**Revenue:** $0 (personal consumption, sharing with friends)
**Knowledge:** Following recipes, learning not to infect the batch
**Customers:** Yourself, flatmates, family at Christmas

**The Vibe:** You bought a kit from Geterbrewed or a local homebrew shop. You're making extract ales in your kitchen. Half the batches are drinkable. Friends say "not bad" politely. You're hooked.

**Key Decisions:** Which kit to buy, following recipes, basic sanitation

---

### Stage 1: Serious Homebrewer (The Enthusiast)
**Scale:** 20-50 litre batches
**Equipment:** All-grain BIAB or cooler mash tun, fermentation chamber (old fridge + temp controller), possibly kegging
**Cost:** $300-1500 total investment
**Revenue:** $0 (still a hobby, but beer is genuinely good now)
**Knowledge:** Understands mash temp, fermentation control, hop schedules. Starting to design own recipes
**Customers:** Friends, BBQs, house parties. "You should sell this!" comments begin

**The Vibe:** You've bought a Grainfather or similar all-in-one electric system. You have a dedicated brewing corner in the garage. You're making beer that's genuinely as good as commercial craft beer. Partners start complaining about the smell. Friends bring you ingredients as gifts.

**Key Decisions:** Recipe design, equipment upgrades, yeast management, water chemistry basics
**Key Milestone:** Making consistently good beer. People choosing your beer over commercial options

---

### Stage 2: The "Wheelbarrow Brewer" (Pre-Commercial)
**Scale:** 50-100 litre batches, multiple brews per month
**Equipment:** Upgraded homebrew rig, possibly 100L pilot system. Multiple fermenters. Kegerator
**Cost:** $1500-5000 investment
**Revenue:** $0 officially (maybe trading/gifting, some illegal cash sales at farmers markets)
**Knowledge:** Recipe refinement through feedback. Interviewing friends/couples about their preferences. Understanding what different people enjoy
**Customers:** Extended friend network, neighbours, local events (informally)

**The Vibe:** You're brewing so much your friends can't drink it all. You're literally loading beer into a wheelbarrow for parties. You start asking people what they like: "Do you prefer hoppy or malty? Light or dark?" You're developing signature recipes based on community feedback. This is the market research phase disguised as generosity.

**Key Decisions:**
- Which 2-3 recipes become your "core range"?
- What does your local community actually want to drink?
- Do you go commercial? (The big decision)
- Business plan, licensing research begins

**The Player-Couples Mechanic:** This is the real-world version of the game's TasteSystem. You're literally interviewing people at BBQs — "Sarah likes wheat beers, Dave wants something hoppy, your mum just wants it to be 'not too strong.'" Building a recipe library informed by real customer preferences.

---

### Stage 3: Nano/Contract Brewery (First Commercial)
**Scale:** 200-1000 litre batches
**Two paths available:**

#### Path A: Contract/Gypsy Brewing
**How it works:** You rent time on someone else's equipment. You bring your recipe, they have the system. You pay per batch.
**Cost:** $2,000-5,000 per batch (varies hugely), plus licensing/company formation
**Advantage:** No capital equipment cost. Focus on brand and sales
**Disadvantage:** No control over scheduling, quality depends on host brewery, margins thin
**Examples:** Many Irish craft brands started this way before getting their own kit

#### Path B: Own Nano Setup
**Equipment:** 200-500L system from suppliers like Brewiks (via Geterbrewed), Chinese systems, or DIY
**Cost:** $15,000-80,000 for equipment + space rental + licensing
**Space:** Small industrial unit, farm outbuilding, converted garage
**Revenue:** $500-5,000/month initially
**Margin:** 60%+ on taproom, 20-30% on wholesale

**Revenue Streams:**
- Direct sales (farmers markets, taproom if you have one)
- Local pub/restaurant accounts (deliver yourself)
- Online sales (limited, logistics heavy)

**Knowledge:** Business operations, licensing (Revenue/HMRC excise), scaling recipes (25L → 500L is NOT linear), packaging, distribution basics

**Key Decisions:**
- Contract brew or own equipment?
- What's the core range? (2-4 beers max)
- Taproom or distribution or both?
- Branding, labels, business identity

**Real Example — Geterbrewed's role:** They're the literal supplier for this transition in Ireland/UK. They sell Brewiks systems from plug-and-play brewpub kits to large-scale commercial. They also provide consultancy — recipe scaling, ingredient sourcing, technical support. They ARE the shop you visit when going from Stage 2 → 3.

---

### Stage 4: Microbrewery (Established Local)
**Scale:** 1,000-5,000 litres per batch, multiple batches/week
**Equipment:** 10-30HL system, multiple fermenters, canning/bottling line, cold room
**Cost:** $100,000-500,000 total investment
**Revenue:** $200,000-2M/year
**Staff:** 2-10 people (brewer, assistant, sales, taproom)
**Space:** Dedicated industrial unit, possibly with taproom

**Revenue Streams:**
- **Taproom** (highest margin: 60%+, 4-5x more profitable per unit than wholesale)
- **Wholesale distribution** (pubs, off-licenses, restaurants)
- **Events** (brewery tours, private hire, beer festivals)
- **Merchandise** (t-shirts, glassware, growlers)
- **Online shop** (direct shipping where legal)

**Knowledge:** Team management, cash flow, distribution logistics, quality consistency at scale, marketing, seasonal planning

**Key Decisions:**
- Expand taproom or push distribution?
- Hire a head brewer vs do everything yourself?
- Core range vs seasonal/experimental?
- Regional distribution deals?

**Real Example — Otterbank Brewing (Muff, Donegal):**
Mixed fermentation and barrel-aged brewery. Small scale but extremely high quality — focused on wild yeast, Brett, barrel aging. Collaborates with other breweries (Third Barrel, Wicklow Wolf). This is the "artisan genius" model: tiny output, enormous reputation, niche expertise. They don't try to be big — they try to be extraordinary.

**Otterbank's model for the game:** This is what the ARTISAN path looks like in practice. Small batches, experimental, barrel-aged, collaborations. Revenue comes from reputation and premium pricing, not volume. They sell what they make and it's always in demand because it's exceptional.

---

### Stage 5: Regional Craft Brewery (Growing Brand)
**Scale:** 5,000-50,000+ litres per batch capacity
**Equipment:** 35HL+ brewhouse, multiple fermentation tanks, automated packaging, lab
**Cost:** $500,000-5M total investment
**Revenue:** $2M-20M/year
**Staff:** 10-50 people
**Space:** Large industrial premises, taproom, possibly hop farm/barrel room

**Revenue Streams:** All of Stage 4, plus:
- **National retail** (supermarket listings — SuperValu, Tesco, etc.)
- **Export** (UK, EU markets)
- **Contract brewing** (brewing for smaller brands — revenue + equipment utilization)
- **Brewery tourism** (destination taproom, tours)
- **Own pub(s)** (highest margin channel)

**Key Decisions:**
- Scale up or stay craft?
- Own pubs vs wholesale distribution?
- Contract brew for others? (Steady revenue but brand dilution risk)
- Sustainability/B-Corp certification?
- Farm/grow own ingredients?

**Real Example — Wicklow Wolf (Newtown Mount Kennedy, Wicklow):**
Founded 2014 by two friends who fell in love with craft beer in California. Now:
- **17,000 sq ft purpose-built brewery** with 35HL Braukon brewhouse
- **10-acre hop farm** in Roundwood — first commercial hop farm in Ireland in 25 years, hand-harvested
- **Taproom** at the brewery
- **B Corp certified** (first Irish brewery, September 2024)
- **120kw solar panels** covering entire brewery roof
- **5,000 native Irish trees** planted on their chemical-free hop farm
- **23 new beers released in 2025** — aggressive innovation
- Distribution across Ireland via O'Briens, CraftCentral, etc.
- Collaborations with breweries like Otterbank

**Wicklow Wolf's model for the game:** This is the ARTISAN path at scale. They grew by being excellent, sustainable, and authentic — not by being cheap or ubiquitous. The hop farm is a late-game feature: growing your own ingredients. The B Corp angle is about brand values, not just beer quality. They prove you can be artisan AND commercially successful.

---

### Stage 6: Pub Empire / Franchise Model
**Scale:** Multiple venues, regional/national distribution
**Equipment:** Production brewery + branded pub chain
**Revenue:** $10M-50M+/year
**Staff:** 50-500+

**Revenue Streams:** All of Stage 5, plus:
- **Owned pubs/bars** (9-11+ venues, each a profit centre)
- **Pub food/restaurant** revenue
- **Brand licensing/franchising** potential
- **Real estate** (pub locations as property investments)

**Key Decisions:**
- How many pubs? Where?
- Manage centrally or let each pub have character?
- Brew exclusively for own pubs or sell to others?
- Franchising vs owned?
- Culture and identity at scale

**Real Example — Galway Bay Brewery:**
Founded 2009 by Jason O'Connell and Niall Walsh — hospitality background, started with a few thousand euro.
- Started as a **single brewpub** (The Oslo Bar, Salthill, Galway)
- Grew to **11 bars** across Galway and Dublin
- Moved to **purpose-built brewery in Oranmore** (40,000 HL capacity)
- Hired key talent: a 21-year-old biotechnology graduate in 2012 who transformed their brewing
- Revenue growth: 3,500 HL (2015) with plans for 40,000 HL
- **Merged with Brú** in 2021 (consolidation)
- Model: brew your own beer, sell it in your own pubs (maximum margin capture)

**Galway Bay's model for the game:** This is the MASS-MARKET path with an Irish twist. They don't franchise — they own. Each pub has character but serves their beer. The margin advantage is enormous: brewing your own beer and selling it in your own pub captures BOTH the production margin AND the retail margin. Compare: selling a keg to a pub for $150 vs selling that same keg through your own bar for $600+.

---

### Stage 7: Global Brand
**Scale:** Millions of litres, international distribution
**Equipment:** Multiple production breweries, international operations
**Revenue:** $100M+/year
**Staff:** 1000+

**Revenue Streams:** Everything, plus:
- **International distribution** (57+ countries for BrewDog)
- **Equity/crowdfunding** (BrewDog's Equity for Punks model)
- **Hotels** (BrewDog DogHouse hotels)
- **Branded experiences** (BrewDog airports, Overworks sour facility)
- **Private label/licensing**
- **TV/media** (BrewDog TV show)
- **Spirits diversification** (BrewDog Distilling Co.)

**Real Example — BrewDog:**
Founded 2007 by James Watt and Martin Dickie, age 24, in Fraserburgh, Scotland.
- Started **filling bottles by hand**, selling from an old van at markets
- **2009-2012:** Rapid growth, opened 6 UK bars, moved to Ellon brewery ($12M investment)
- **Equity for Punks** crowdfunding: raised $100M+ from 100,000+ investors worldwide in multiple rounds. First crowdfunding scheme of its kind for a brewery
- **2014:** "Broke" America, first international bar (Stockholm)
- **2017:** TSG Consumer Partners bought 22% stake for ~$280M (implied $1.3B valuation)
- **Growth:** 37 employees → 1,247 employees (2010-2018). Revenue $5M → $225M
- **2023:** Available in 57 countries, 129+ bars worldwide
- **Controversies:** Workplace culture issues, founder behaviour — cautionary tale about growth at all costs

**BrewDog's model for the game:** The MASS-MARKET path taken to the extreme. Marketing-driven, brand-first, scale-obsessed. The crowdfunding mechanic is interesting for the game (community investment). But the cautionary lesson: grow too fast and you lose what made you special. The game should let players pursue this path but show the trade-offs (staff morale, quality perception, authenticity reputation).

## Revenue Comparison Table

| Stage | Monthly Revenue | Best Margin Channel | Key Expense |
|-------|----------------|--------------------|----|
| 0-1 Kitchen/Hobbyist | $0 | N/A | Ingredients ($50-100/batch) |
| 2 Wheelbarrow | $0 (informal) | Word of mouth | Equipment ($100-500/batch) |
| 3 Nano/Contract | $500-5K | Taproom/markets (60%) | Equipment/rent ($2-5K/month) |
| 4 Microbrewery | $15-150K | Taproom (60%+) | Staff + rent ($5-20K/month) |
| 5 Regional | $150K-1.5M | Own pubs (65%+) | Distribution + staff ($50K+/month) |
| 6 Pub Empire | $1M-5M | Own pub chain (65%+) | Real estate + staff ($200K+/month) |
| 7 Global | $10M+ | All channels | Everything ($1M+/month) |

## Revenue Channel Margins (Critical for Game Economy)

| Channel | Margin | Why |
|---------|--------|-----|
| **Taproom** | 60-65% | Direct to consumer, no middleman. A half-barrel = $600 taproom vs $150 wholesale |
| **Own Pub** | 65%+ | Even better than taproom — full food/drink revenue |
| **Events/Tourism** | 50-60% | Premium pricing, experiential |
| **Wholesale/Pubs** | 20-30% | Distributor + retailer take their cut |
| **Retail/Supermarket** | 15-25% | Volume but thin margin. Listing fees, promotions |
| **Contract Brewing (for others)** | 10-20% | Low margin but steady utilization of equipment |
| **Export** | 10-20% | Shipping, tariffs, import partners |

## Mapping to BeerBrew Tycoon's Current Systems

### Current Game Stages vs Reality

| Game Stage | Real Equivalent | Accuracy |
|-----------|----------------|----------|
| Garage | Stage 0-1 (Kitchen/Hobbyist) | Good |
| Microbrewery | Stage 3-4 (Nano/Micro) | Skips Stage 2 "wheelbarrow" |
| Artisan path | Stage 4-5 (Otterbank/Wicklow Wolf model) | Good concept, needs more depth |
| Mass-Market path | Stage 6-7 (Galway Bay/BrewDog model) | Good concept, needs more depth |

### What's Missing

1. **The "Wheelbarrow" Stage** — The informal pre-commercial phase where you're building recipes based on community feedback. This is actually a crucial and fun game stage: brewing for friends, getting their taste preferences, developing signature recipes. In Game Dev Tycoon terms, this is like making games for yourself before publishing.

2. **Contract/Gypsy Brewing** — A real and important path. You could brew using someone else's equipment before buying your own. Lower risk, lower margin, faster to market. This could be an alternative to the expansion mechanic.

3. **The Taproom as Primary Revenue** — Current game has "taproom" as a distribution channel with 1.0x margin. In reality, taproom is the MOST profitable channel by far (4-5x wholesale). The game should make taproom the default and best channel, with wholesale being a volume play at lower margins.

4. **Own Pub(s)** — Galway Bay's model. Buying/opening your own pub is the endgame margin capture. Each pub is a profit centre that exclusively serves your beer.

5. **Hop Farm / Ingredient Sourcing** — Wicklow Wolf's model. Growing your own hops is a late-game investment that reduces costs, improves freshness, and adds authenticity/brand value.

6. **Collaborations** — Otterbank + Wicklow Wolf + Third Barrel. Brewing with other breweries is a real revenue and reputation driver. In-game: temporary partnership that produces a unique beer with combined bonuses.

7. **Barrel Aging Program** — Otterbank's specialty. Buying barrels (wine, whiskey, rum), aging beer for months. High cost, high reward, very artisan. Ties into the conditioning mechanic.

8. **The Geterbrewed Equivalent** — The game should have a supplier/consultant NPC or shop that helps with the transition. "Ready to go commercial? Here's what you need."

## Recommendations for Game Design

### New/Modified Expansion Stages

| Stage | Name | Unlocks | Revenue Streams |
|-------|------|---------|-----------------|
| 0 | **Kitchen** | Extract brewing, simple recipes | $0 — friends' feedback only |
| 1 | **Garage Brewery** | All-grain, recipe design, competitions | Farmers market stalls |
| 2 | **Community Brewer** | Taste testing events, recipe refinement via feedback | Informal sales, word of mouth |
| 3A | **Contract Brewer** | Use others' equipment, focus on brand | Wholesale only, thin margin |
| 3B | **Nano Brewery** | Own small system (200-500L) | Taproom + local wholesale |
| 4 | **Microbrewery** | Larger system, staff, distribution | Taproom + wholesale + events |
| 5A | **Artisan Brewery** | Barrel program, hop farm, collabs, B-Corp | Premium pricing, tourism, reputation |
| 5B | **Pub Chain** | Own pubs, high margin, branded venues | Own pubs + wholesale + retail |
| 6A | **Artisan Legend** (Endgame) | Like Otterbank/Wicklow Wolf | Global reputation, limited runs, farm-to-glass |
| 6B | **Brewing Empire** (Endgame) | Like Galway Bay/BrewDog | National/international, 10+ pubs, massive revenue |

### New Mechanics Suggested

1. **Community Feedback System** (Stage 2) — Interview NPCs about their taste preferences. Build recipes to match. This IS the game's taste discovery system applied to customers rather than your own palate.

2. **Contract Brewing Option** (Stage 3) — Pay per batch to use a larger system. Lower margin but no equipment cost. Good for testing recipes at commercial scale before committing capital.

3. **Taproom Management** (Stage 3+) — Your most profitable channel. Staff it, run events, build a local following. Each upgrade (seating, food menu, live music) increases revenue.

4. **Collaboration Brews** (Stage 4+) — Partner with another brewery (NPC). Combine strengths for a unique limited release. Reputation boost + unique beer.

5. **Barrel Aging Program** (Stage 5A) — Buy barrels, age beer for multiple turns. High cost, occupies fermenter space, but produces premium products. The Otterbank mechanic.

6. **Hop Farm** (Stage 5A) — Late-game investment. Costs capital + turns to establish. Reduces ingredient costs, adds freshness bonus, unique "estate" beers. The Wicklow Wolf mechanic.

7. **Pub Acquisition** (Stage 5B) — Buy pub locations. Each is a profit centre with 65% margin. Staff them, choose which beers to serve. The Galway Bay mechanic.

8. **Brand/Marketing** (Stage 5B+) — Merchandise, social media presence, beer ratings. Affects demand multiplier. The BrewDog mechanic (without the controversy).
